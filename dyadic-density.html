The Density of Dyadic Numbers on \(\mathbb{R}\)

The set of dyadic numbers is defined as \( D = \left\{ \frac{k}{2^n} \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}, n \in \mathbb{N} \right\} \). It is similar to the set of decimal numbers \( \mathbb{D} \), but in base 2.

Theorem Density of \( D \) in \( \mathbb{R} \):
The set of dyadic numbers \( D \) is dense in \( \mathbb{R} \).

Proof

For every \( n \in \mathbb{N} \), define \( x_n = \frac{\lfloor 2^n x \rfloor}{2^n} \in D \).
Then, as \( n \to \infty \), \( x_n \to x \) by the squeeze theorem.

Application

Find all continuous functions \( f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \) two times differentiable at 0 such that \( f(0) = 1 \) and \( f(2x) = 2f(x)^2 - 1 \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \).

Solution

Analysis shows that either \( f(x) \in [-1, 1] \) or \( f(x) \geq 1 \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \).

Case 1: \( f(x) \in [-1, 1] \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \)

Let \( d \in D \). For any dyadic \( d = \frac{k}{2^n} \), the range of \( f \) implies there exists \( a_d \in \mathbb{R} \) such that \( f(d) = \cos(a_d d) \). The equation gives \( f(d/2) = \cos(a_d d / 2) \), and by induction \( f\left(\frac{d}{2^m}\right) = \cos\left(\frac{a_d d}{2^m}\right) \) for all \( m \in \mathbb{N} \). So for a fixed \( d \), on the "comb" of values \( C_{d} = \left\{ \frac{d}{2^m}, m \in \mathbb{N} \right\} \), \( a_d \) is constant.

Since \( f \) is two times differentiable at 0, we examine the limit of the difference quotient along this sequence \( h_m = \frac{d}{2^m} \) as \( m \to \infty \) with d fixed: \[ \frac{f(h_m) - f(0)}{h_m^2} \xrightarrow[m \to \infty]{} \frac{f''(0)}{2} \] Given \( f(h_m) = \cos(a_d h_m) \), the Taylor expansion at 0 gives: \[ \frac{\cos(a_d h_m) - 1}{h_m^2} = \frac{(1 - \frac{(a_d h_m)^2}{2} + o(h_m^2)) - 1}{h_m^2} \xrightarrow[m \to \infty]{} -\frac{a_d^2}{2} \]

Comparing the limits, we get \( -\frac{a_d^2}{2} = \frac{f''(0)}{2} \), which means \( a_d^2 = -f''(0) \). Since \( f''(0) \) is a constant independent of \( d \), it follows that \( a_d^2 \) (and thus \( a_d \)) is the same for all dyadic numbers. Let this constant be \( a \).

Thus, \( f(d) = \cos(ad) \) for all \( d \in D \). By the density of dyadic numbers and the continuity of \( f \), we conclude: \[ \forall x \in \mathbb{R}, f(x) = \cos(ax) \]

Case 2: \( f(x) \geq 1 \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \)

By a similar argument using \( \cosh \) instead of \( \cos \), there exists \( a \in \mathbb{R} \) such that \( f(x) = \cosh(ax) \).

Conclusion The set of solutions is: \[ S = \left\{ x \mapsto \cos(ax) \mid a \in \mathbb{R} \right\} \cup \left\{ x \mapsto \cosh(ax) \mid a \in \mathbb{R} \right\} \]

Bonus Application: Another functional Equation

Let \( g \) be a continuous function such that \( g(2x) = 4g(x) \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \).

Induction \( P(n) \) We show by induction that for all \( n \in \mathbb{N} \): \[ \forall k \in \mathbb{Z}, \quad g\left(\frac{k}{2^n}\right) = \frac{g(1) \cdot k^2}{2^{2n}} \] Base case (\(n=0\)): \( g(k) = k^2 g(1) \).
Inductive step (\(n \to n+1\)): \( \text{Let k }\ \in \mathbb{Z}, g\left(\frac{k}{2^{n+1}}\right) = \frac{1}{4}g\left(\frac{k}{2^n}\right) = \frac{g(1)k^2}{2^{2n+2}}= \frac{g(1)k^2}{2^{2(n+1)}} \).

By density of \( D \) and continuity of \( g \), we conclude \( g(x) = g(1)x^2 \) for all \( x \in \mathbb{R} \).

Generalization: \(\alpha\)-adic Numbers

The same reasoning can be applied to any base \(\alpha \in \mathbb{N} \setminus \{0, 1\}\). The set of \(\alpha\)-adic numbers \( D_\alpha = \{ \frac{k}{\alpha^n} \mid k \in \mathbb{Z}, n \in \mathbb{N} \} \) is dense in \(\mathbb{R}\).

Consider the functional equation \( g(px) = p^{\alpha} g(x) \) for a fixed \( \alpha \in \mathbb{N}^* \) and \( g \) continuous. Using the density of \( D_\alpha \), we can show that for all \( d \in D_\alpha \): \[ g(d) = g(1) d^{\alpha} \] By continuity, this property extends to the entire real line, yielding the general solution: \[ g(x) = K x^{\alpha} \quad (\text{where } K = g(1)) \]

This demonstrates a profound link between the algebraic structure of a functional equation and the topological density of its "natural" number system. The base \(\alpha\) of the equation dictates which dense subset of \(\mathbb{R}\) is best suited to bridge the gap between discrete points and continuous functions.
